Endogenous product attribute choice and the design of electric vehicle subsidies (Job Market Paper)

Kevin Remmy
September 2020

Abstract

This paper shows how firms in multi-product oligopolies react to subsidies when their response can be multi-dimensional. I do so in the setting of electric vehicles in Germany. I build and estimate a structural model of demand and supply of the new car market where firms maximize profits by choosing the price and the driving range of electric cars. Evaluating different subsidy schemes used around the world, I find that policy outcomes are mutually exclusive and generate distributional effects. Different subsidy schemes optimize consumer surplus, CO2 emissions form new cars and diffusion. Subsidies can induce firms to undertake two strategies: Selling more expensive cars with a higher range on which they collect a higher markup or selling cheaper cars at a lower range capturing more consumers but yielding a lower markup. These two different strategies generate different substitution patterns, delivering different outcomes. My estimation also suggests that the marginal cost of providing range has decreased by 39% from 2012 to 2018, inducing firms to sell more expensive electric cars with a higher range. Finally, I evaluate the distributional effects of different subsidy schemes and find that there exists considerable heterogeneity in preferences for different subsidy schemes across income groups. I show how targeting different income groups can be compatible with achieving specific policy objectives.
